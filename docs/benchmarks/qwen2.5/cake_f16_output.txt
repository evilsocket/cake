The theory of general relativity is a scientific explanation for gravity that was developed in the mid-20th century. The theory was proposed by Albert Einstein, but its full meaning went beyond scientific discovery to encompass physical phenomena that could be observed and understood with modern technology.
At its core, general relativity states that gravity is caused by the warping of space-time caused by mass and energy. This means that if you try to push on or pull on a massive object, the same force pulls on everything else in the area. 
In addition to this basic idea, general relativity also has several other important predictions: it helps us to explain the bending of light around celestial bodies like galaxies, how planets move in orbits, and even why the Earth rotates on its axis every 24 hours - all of these are examples of phenomena that defy mathematical explanation.

It's worth noting, however, that not everyone agrees with the theory's basic tenets and may not fully embrace the implications it predicts for physics and astronomy. For example, some believe that gravity is just a mathematical artifact, rather than an inherent reality, or that its effects must be accounted for according to our current cosmological understanding.
Ultimately, the idea that the universe is filled with wormholes connecting different points across spacetime has led many physicists to speculate that general relativity might be one day prove false!
